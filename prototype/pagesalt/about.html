Idea

We are trying to eliminate the noise and unneeded surrounding, taking open-source fonts out of their current cluttered context and present them on a well designed and functional type-foundry-like platform. The project is run by the collective Stefan Endress and Alastair O’Shea.

The ‘open’ in open-foundry stands for open-source, free and easily available. The word ‘foundry’ is an extraction of the term ‘type foundry’, that connotes the idea of professional quality, industrial heritage and a cutting edge selection process.

Problem

If you search for ‘free web fonts’ on google, one of the first search result is ‘Google Fonts’ and the link description is ‘Hundreds of free, open-source fonts optimised for the web. Just 3 quick steps between you and a good lookin' website.’. Very promising, but the major problem is already in the description ‘hundreds’ and ‘3 quick steps’. It seems fairly easy to find a good looking font with the various filter options like classification, thickness and slant or sorting by popularity, but it isn’t. What is missing is the human touch and a professional eye.

Jan Tschichold (2 April 1902 Leipzig, Germany – 11 August 1974 Locarno, Switzerland) was a master in typography and his understanding of typography was straightforward:

The best typography is invisible to the reader and serves to transmit the thoughts and intent of the author. Beautiful text, a text well-composed, is legible. One of the highest virtues of good typography is its subtle elegance. It is not the duty of the typographer to consciously display or emulate the style of current trends, not to reflect the spirit of the times. Typography must be itself. It must be pleasing to the eye and not tiring. Good typography has absolutely nothing to do with remarkable or exotic type styles. This is the opinion of amateurs. The essence of letterform is not modernity, but readability.

Tschichold's understanding of typography may now be outdated, but it illustrates well that the choice of the font and its style is never arbitrary and should always be itself.

Most of the web services that offer free web fonts are poorly designed, create a poor user experience and focus on ‘exotic’ type styles and novelty fonts without having enough functionality to test them. It is difficult to separate the good from the bad, the amateurs from the professionals.

Solution

Our idea is to offer a carefully controlled number of fonts in the most clear and dynamic way possible, which doesn’t overwhelm the user, but provides enough interest and excitement to encourage further exploration.
